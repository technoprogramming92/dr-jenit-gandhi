BARIATRIC SURGERY

The only established option for achieving long-term weight loss is bariatric surgery, also known as obesity surgery. It also significantly improves most related disorders, such as diabetes, high blood pressure, and high cholesterol, and lowers the overall risk of heart disease. Most bariatric or obesity surgical operations result in 60-75% excess weight loss. This is determined by the type of weight reduction surgery performed and the dedication of both the patient and the team.

Preparing for surgery

For at least one week prior to surgery, you will follow a high protein diet. Based on your medical history, you may need to see another specialist before surgery, such as an endocrinologist, nephrologist, or chest physician. It is best to be admitted in the evening before the procedure.

Expected weight loss after Bariatric Surgery

Bariatric surgery, sometimes known as weight loss surgery, causes significant weight loss. Most individuals will lose between 65 and 75% of their excess weight following any of the aforementioned surgeries. This could be less or more depending on a variety of factors, including post-operative diet, exercise attempts, bariatric team follow-up, associated co-morbidities, drug intake, and so forth. Surgery significantly improves the patient's quality of life, and most patients are able to retain at least 50% of their excess weight loss 5 years after surgery, which is substantially higher than any other kind of weight loss.

Post-operative schedule for follow up

Regular follow-up is essential for success after bariatric surgery. Following surgery, you are expected to meet with the doctor and the team at least three times in the first year to review your reports, and once a year thereafter. There are numerous adjustments in your lifestyle, eating habits, and exercise program that necessitate ongoing support and counseling. Patients who follow up on a regular basis are more likely to achieve positive outcomes.
