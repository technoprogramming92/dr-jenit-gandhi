Pancreatic Cancer

Pancreatic cancer surgery is a crucial treatment option for patients diagnosed with pancreatic cancer, aiming to remove the tumor and prevent the spread of the disease. The surgery's complexity depends on the tumor's location, size, and involvement with surrounding organs and blood vessels.

Types of Pancreatic Cancer Surgery

1. Whipple Procedure (Pancreaticoduodenectomy):
  • The most common surgery for tumors in the head of the pancreas.
  • Involves removing the head of the pancreas, part of the small intestine, gallbladder, and bile duct.
  • Sometimes part of the stomach and nearby lymph nodes are also removed.
2. Distal Pancreatectomy:
  • This surgery is for tumors located in the body and tail of the pancreas.
  • Involves removing the tail and/or body of the pancreas, often along with the spleen.
3. Total Pancreatectomy:
  • In rare cases, the entire pancreas may need to be removed.
  • This procedure is performed when the cancer is spread throughout the pancreas.

Eligibility for Surgery

Not all patients with pancreatic cancer are candidates for surgery. The decision depends on the cancer’s stage, location, and whether it has spread to other parts of the body. Pre-surgical evaluations including imaging tests and biopsy help determine the feasibility and type of surgery required.

Risks and Complications

Pancreatic cancer surgery, like any major surgery, carries risks including infection, bleeding, and digestive issues. Specific complications might include delayed gastric emptying, pancreatic fistula, or diabetes if the entire pancreas is removed.

Recovery and Aftercare

Post-surgery recovery involves a hospital stay where the patient is closely monitored. Gradual reintroduction of food and fluids is necessary, and patients may need nutritional support and enzyme supplements. Full recovery can take weeks to months, depending on the procedure and the patient's overall health.
