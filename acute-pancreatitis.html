Acute Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis is a sudden inflammation of the pancreas that can cause severe abdominal pain and other complications. Surgery for acute pancreatitis is often considered when there is a need to remove infected or dead tissue, drain abscesses, or address complications that cannot be managed by medication alone.

When is Surgery Needed?

Surgery for acute pancreatitis is typically recommended in the following situations:

  • Necrotizing Pancreatitis: When parts of the pancreas die due to lack of blood supply, surgery may be necessary to remove the dead tissue.
  • Pancreatic Abscess: If a collection of pus forms in the pancreas, it needs to be drained surgically to prevent infection from spreading.
  • Gallstone-Induced Pancreatitis: In cases where gallstones cause the pancreatitis, surgery might involve removing the gallbladder (cholecystectomy) to prevent future attacks.
  • Persistent Symptoms: If symptoms persist despite conservative treatments, surgery may be required to address the underlying issue.

Types of Surgery

  • Debridement: This involves the removal of dead or infected pancreatic tissue.
  • Drainage Procedures: For abscesses or fluid collections, drains may be surgically placed to remove the buildup.
  • Cholecystectomy: If gallstones are the cause, the gallbladder is removed.
  • Minimally Invasive Surgery: Laparoscopic techniques may be used to reduce recovery time and minimize scarring.

Preparation for Surgery

Before undergoing surgery for acute pancreatitis, Dr. Jenit Gandhi will perform a thorough evaluation, including imaging studies like CT scans or MRIs, to assess the extent of the disease. You may need to fast for a certain period before the procedure, and preoperative medications or antibiotics may be prescribed.

As with any surgery, there are risks involved, including bleeding, infection, and potential damage to surrounding organs. Dr. Gandhi will discuss these risks with you and provide strategies to minimize them.
